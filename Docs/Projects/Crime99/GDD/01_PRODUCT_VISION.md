## Purpose

This document defines the long-term product vision.

It describes:

* what the game should become;
* which experience it should create;
* who it is for;
* what makes it distinct;
* which product directions are important;
* which directions should be avoided.

It does not define the complete prototype scope.

The authoritative first-prototype boundaries are maintained in:

[`16_PROTOTYPE_SCOPE.md`](16_PROTOTYPE_SCOPE.md)

## Vision Statement

The project is an original urban action game built around short, chaotic, highly replayable sessions.

Players move through a reactive city, steal vehicles, fight rivals, exploit traffic and police pressure, create spectacular destruction, and improvise with whatever opportunities the environment provides.

The game should capture the immediacy, readability, and anarchic pleasure associated with classic elevated-camera crime-action games while establishing its own identity through:

* modern controls;
* stronger vehicle interaction;
* creative scoring;
* reactive city systems;
* solo play with bots;
* multiplayer-focused modes;
* a distinct Neon over Dirt visual direction;
* long-term faction-based conflict.

## Core Product Identity

The project is primarily:

> A fast urban action game for small groups of friends that remains fully playable and enjoyable alone against bots.

Its main identity comes from the combination of:

* vehicles as temporary weapons and tools;
* fast switching between foot combat and driving;
* short accessible matches;
* strong environmental interaction;
* visible city reactions;
* player-created chaos;
* competitive and cooperative modes;
* high replayability through emergent situations.

The project should not depend on:

* a large narrative campaign;
* a persistent open world;
* large public player populations;
* complex simulation for its own sake;
* detailed realism;
* manual micromanagement;
* permanent power progression.

## Emotional Target

The desired emotional response is a mix of:

* nostalgia;
* speed;
* tension;
* surprise;
* dark humour;
* tactical improvisation;
* destructive satisfaction;
* social chaos.

A good match should create memorable stories such as:

* escaping one player only to collide with another;
* abandoning a burning car moments before it explodes;
* turning police attention against a rival;
* triggering a large chain reaction;
* stealing the vehicle that was about to kill the player;
* surviving a fight through a quick vehicle switch;
* winning through an unusual environmental kill;
* seeing several city systems interact unexpectedly but understandably.

These moments should emerge naturally from gameplay.

## Nostalgia in a New Form

Nostalgia is an important part of the product identity.

The goal is not to recreate an old game exactly.

The goal is to recreate specific feelings in a modern form:

* immediate access;
* readable urban chaos;
* fast vehicle theft;
* compact maps;
* police escalation;
* arcade violence;
* exaggerated city life;
* short sessions that encourage replay;
* social stories created through unpredictable conflict.

The project should feel familiar in spirit but original in execution.

## Originality Boundary

This project is an original intellectual property.

It is not:

* a remake;
* a sequel;
* a fan game;
* a recreation;
* an adaptation;
* a spiritual successor that depends on copied content.

No protected content should be copied, including:

* names;
* characters;
* factions;
* cities;
* maps;
* missions;
* dialogue;
* visual assets;
* UI;
* audio;
* logos;
* vehicle designs;
* story beats;
* fictional brands.

References to classic games are used only to discuss high-level genre feelings and design principles.

The final product must have:

* its own name;
* its own setting;
* its own factions;
* its own visual identity;
* its own humour;
* its own game modes;
* its own world logic.

## Player Fantasy

The player is an opportunistic urban outlaw operating in a city full of conflict and opportunity.

The player fantasy includes:

* moving freely between foot and vehicle gameplay;
* stealing whatever vehicle is useful;
* using the city itself as a weapon;
* improvising under pressure;
* turning danger into advantage;
* escalating a small incident into major chaos;
* escaping before the situation becomes unmanageable;
* defeating rivals through skill, timing, or creativity;
* influencing larger conflicts without managing them directly.

The player should not feel like:

* a military commander;
* a city manager;
* a passive observer;
* a realistic criminal simulator participant;
* a character locked into one vehicle or class;
* a player required to grind permanent power.

## Target Audience

## Primary Audience

The primary audience includes:

* players with nostalgia for classic elevated-camera crime-action games;
* players who enjoy arcade driving and destruction;
* small friend groups looking for accessible multiplayer;
* players who prefer short replayable sessions;
* players who enjoy emergent situations;
* players who like both direct combat and environmental creativity.

## Secondary Audience

The secondary audience includes:

* solo players who enjoy competent bots;
* players who avoid public matchmaking;
* players interested in reactive city systems;
* players who enjoy cooperative modes;
* players who prefer premium games over aggressive monetisation;
* players who value gameplay readability over realism.

## Accessibility of Entry

The game should be easy to understand at a basic level.

A new player should quickly grasp:

* how to move;
* how to aim;
* how to steal a vehicle;
* how to shoot;
* how to earn points;
* how police attention works;
* how to respawn;
* how to recognise danger.

Mastery should come from:

* vehicle control;
* weapon timing;
* positioning;
* route knowledge;
* environmental awareness;
* managing police attention;
* creative kill opportunities;
* understanding other players;
* using city systems tactically.

The game should be:

> easy to enter, difficult to master.

## Tone

The tone is:

* adult;
* criminal;
* exaggerated;
* dirty;
* cynical;
* darkly humorous;
* energetic;
* occasionally absurd.

The tone should avoid:

* realistic suffering as spectacle;
* disturbing detail;
* serious crime simulation;
* moral lecturing;
* family-friendly softness;
* constant parody;
* humour that destroys tension.

Violence should be stylised and readable.

The game can include:

* blood;
* explosions;
* police chases;
* gang conflict;
* vehicle impacts;
* destruction;
* cynical fictional advertising;
* exaggerated city behaviour;
* dark situational humour.

## Session Experience

The game should support short and medium-length sessions.

### Short Modes

Typical duration:

* short configurable sessions whose exact duration is `Needs Prototype Validation`.

Examples:

* Free-for-All;
* Team Deathmatch;
* Golden Car;
* Most Value Collected;
* King of the Road.

Purpose:

* immediate access;
* quick replay;
* party-friendly sessions;
* low commitment;
* rapid experimentation.

### Larger Modes

Typical duration:

* duration is `Deferred` and has no confirmed numeric range.

Examples:

* Faction War;
* larger cooperative battles;
* more strategic objective modes.

Purpose:

* stronger match narrative;
* territory conflict;
* team identity;
* escalating pressure;

* more persistent tactical decisions within a single match.

Exact durations remain subject to testing.

## Solo and Multiplayer Vision

The product is multiplayer-focused but must not be multiplayer-dependent.

## Solo Play

Core modes should support bots.

Solo play should provide:

* complete matches;
* meaningful challenge;
* varied situations;
* adjustable bot counts;
* useful practice;
* replayability;
* freedom from public matchmaking.

Bots are not only a fallback.

They are a core product feature.

## Multiplayer

Human players should increase:

* unpredictability;
* social tension;
* tactical deception;
* rivalry;
* humour;
* story generation.

Long-term multiplayer directions may include:

* private lobbies;
* public lobbies;
* Quick Play;
* LAN-style local access;
* bot replacement;
* late joining;
* reconnect support;
* adjustable session rules.

These features remain deferred until the solo-testable foundation is proven.

## Core Mode Direction

## Free-for-All Deathmatch

Free-for-All is the first prototype mode.

It tests:

* core controls;
* combat;
* vehicles;
* bots;
* creative scoring;
* city pressure;
* respawning;
* replayability.

It should remain a supported long-term mode.

## Team Deathmatch

Team Deathmatch should extend the validated Free-for-All foundation.

It should require minimal new fundamental mechanics.

Its purpose is:

* team play;
* accessible social competition;
* preparation for more complex faction systems.

## Faction War

Faction War is the major long-term identity mode.

It should combine:

* player combat;
* vehicles;
* building control;
* influence networks;
* faction NPCs;
* income;
* upkeep;
* player-funded upgrades;
* local support;
* elimination pressure;
* police pressure against dominant factions.

Faction War should remain an action game mode.

It must not become:

* an RTS;
* a base-building game;
* a spreadsheet economy;
* a micromanagement game.

Players should influence the larger conflict through direct action.

## Cooperative Play

Cooperative modes should reuse:

* combat systems;
* vehicle systems;
* bots;
* police;
* map systems;
* destruction;
* later faction logic.

Coop should support players who prefer collaboration over direct competition.

## Additional Mode Potential

Possible later modes include:

* Zombie Outbreak;
* Golden Car;
* Most Value Collected;
* King of the Road.

These are opportunities, not commitments.

Any new mode should:

* reuse existing systems;
* create a distinct match rhythm;
* remain understandable;
* support bots where practical;
* avoid requiring a separate game architecture.

## City Vision

The city is a reactive gameplay system.

It should not behave like a static arena.

The city includes:

* roads;
* traffic;
* pedestrians;
* police;
* vehicles;
* buildings;
* hazards;
* destructible objects;
* changing local density;
* later faction-controlled locations.

The city should react to:

* gunfire;
* explosions;
* vehicle collisions;
* police pursuit;
* player concentration;
* local danger.

Reactions should be:

* visible;
* understandable;
* useful to gameplay;
* limited enough to avoid simulation overload.

The city should create opportunities but should not dominate the player.

## Player Priority

Players and combat bots remain the main actors.

Traffic, civilians, police, and later faction NPCs exist to:

* support;
* complicate;
* amplify;
* redirect;
* contextualise

player conflict.

The project should avoid situations where:

* police are the main enemy most of the time;
* NPC factions win without player involvement;
* traffic blocks all meaningful action;
* city systems obscure player decisions;
* simulation becomes more important than control quality.

## Vehicle Vision

Vehicles are central to the product identity.

They are:

* transport;
* temporary protection;
* weapons;
* escape tools;
* hazards;
* sources of explosion;
* resources to steal and abandon.

Vehicles should feel:

* distinct;
* powerful;
* disposable;
* readable;
* easy to enter;
* risky to keep when damaged.

The game should encourage frequent switching rather than attachment to one permanent vehicle.

Long-term vehicle expansion may include:

* more classes;
* motorcycles;
* specialised faction vehicles;
* police vehicles;
* military vehicles;
* rare vehicles;
* cosmetic variation.

The product should not become a realistic driving simulator.

## Combat Vision

Combat should be readable and physical.

The intended qualities are:

* clear damage;
* strong sound;
* visible hit feedback;
* medium time to kill;
* simple health and armour;
* distinct weapon roles;
* strong close-range danger;
* environmental opportunities.

The game should avoid:

* detailed hit zones;
* realistic ballistic simulation;
* excessive weapon inventory;
* long tactical downtime;
* complex cover systems;
* slow military-style combat.

## Creative Chaos

Creative kills are a major part of the long-term identity.

The game should recognise actions such as:

* roadkills;
* vehicle explosions;
* chain reactions;
* environmental kills;
* police-assisted kills;
* multi-stage destruction;
* unusual weapon and vehicle combinations.

Creative actions should receive:

* score;
* readable labels;
* strong sound;
* prominent visual feedback;
* memorable statistics.

The system should encourage creativity without making standard combat irrelevant.

## Police and Escalation Vision

Police provide escalating external pressure.

Personal escalation should feel temporary and reactive.

Long-term escalation may include:

* multiple wanted levels;
* increasing police response;
* stronger vehicle pursuit;
* tactical police presence;
* military response at extreme levels.

Police should be:

* a threat;
* a resource to manipulate;
* a force that can be redirected;
* a pressure against reckless behaviour.

They should not permanently dominate the match.

Faction-level attention may later provide soft balancing pressure against dominant teams.

## Faction Vision

Long-term factions should create identity and strategic structure.

Players should:

* join fixed factions;
* fight for buildings;
* support expansion;
* influence NPC strength;
* gain income;
* pay upkeep indirectly;
* fund upgrades;
* defend connected territory;
* exploit isolated enemy areas.

Faction NPCs should:

* defend;
* move locally;
* expand slowly;
* create visible front lines;
* support player action.

They should not:

* require direct orders;
* replace players;
* create large management interfaces;
* turn the mode into a strategy game.

## Progression Philosophy

The project should prioritise match-based progression over permanent power.

Within a match, players may gain:

* weapons;
* armour;
* vehicles;
* money;
* access;
* influence;
* lives;
* tactical options.

Between matches, progression should avoid pay-to-win.

Possible long-term progression may include:

* cosmetics;
* statistics;
* titles;
* achievements;
* unlockable visual options;
* sidegrades;
* profile progression.

Permanent competitive advantages should be avoided.

## Visual Identity

The long-term visual direction is:

> Neon over Dirt.

The city should combine:

* old infrastructure;
* industrial decay;
* wet streets;
* grime;
* soot;
* smoke;
* selective neon;
* strong vehicle lights;
* police lights;
* readable silhouettes;
* slightly exaggerated proportions.

The game should not look:

* clean;
* futuristic;
* cyberpunk by default;
* toy-like;
* photorealistic;
* assembled from unrelated asset packs.

Neon is an accent.

The city remains dirty, aged, and functional.

## Camera Identity

The current intended camera is:

* elevated;
* fixed in world orientation;
* moderately tilted;
* non-rotating;
* closer on foot;
* farther in vehicles;
* farther at high speed.

The camera should support:

* spatial understanding;
* quick combat reading;
* high-speed driving;
* visible city density;
* nostalgic genre recognition;
* modern 3D presentation.

This remains a prototype assumption until validated.

## Audio Identity

Audio should create strong physical impact.

Important sound priorities include:

* vehicle collisions;
* roadkills;
* gunfire;
* explosions;
* engine differences;
* police pressure;
* environmental ambience.

The desired impact is:

* heavy;
* bass-driven;
* direct;
* arcade-like;
* readable.

Music should support:

* urban tension;
* speed;
* dirt;
* neon;
* criminal energy.

The prototype may use only one track, but the long-term game may require a broader soundtrack identity.

## Production Vision

The project is designed for AI-orchestrated production.

Human work should focus on:

* vision;
* review;
* feedback;
* approval;
* prioritisation;
* design decisions.

Routine production should be handled through:

* AI coding agents;
* AI-assisted asset generation;
* AI-assisted Blender workflows;
* modular assets;
* procedural methods;
* Store assets where efficient;
* automated validation;
* controlled documentation.

The production model succeeds only if:

* assets are reproducible;
* code remains reviewable;
* styles remain consistent;
* regular manual human implementation is unnecessary;
* quality can scale.

The project should change its production approach if a pipeline repeatedly requires manual repair.

## Platform Direction

The current tendency is:

* Windows desktop prototype;
* Steam as the likely first commercial platform.

Possible later directions include:

* additional desktop platforms;
* console;
* browser;
* crossplay.

These are not confirmed.

The prototype should not compromise its core experience to satisfy every possible platform.

## Commercial Direction

The current tendency is:

* premium purchase;
* price is `Deferred` with no confirmed numeric assumption;
* no pay-to-win;
* possible cosmetic expansion later.

The commercial product should provide enough value through:

* replayability;
* modes;
* bots;
* multiplayer;
* city variation;
* vehicle and combat depth;
* faction systems;
* visual identity.

Exact pricing and Early Access scope remain open.

## Early Access Direction

Faction War is expected to be important for a convincing Early Access offer.

Free-for-All and Team Deathmatch alone may not sufficiently communicate the long-term project identity.

A possible development direction is:

1. validate solo Free-for-All;
2. validate real multiplayer;
3. extend to Team Deathmatch;
4. build a reduced Faction War;
5. scale Faction War;
6. add cooperative and alternative modes.

This sequence is directional, not yet an approved production commitment.

## Product Success Conditions

The product vision is successful if the game can provide:

* satisfying movement;
* satisfying driving;
* strong foot-versus-vehicle interaction;
* replayable short matches;
* useful bots;
* creative destruction;
* readable city reactions;
* distinct visual identity;
* multiplayer potential;
* scalable AI-assisted production.

## Product Failure Conditions

The project should be reconsidered if:

* driving cannot become enjoyable;
* the camera cannot support speed;
* vehicles make foot combat irrelevant;
* bots cannot sustain matches;
* city systems only create frustration;
* the style is unreadable in motion;
* assets require routine manual production;
* the game remains interesting only because it resembles another property;
* long-term modes require a fundamentally different game.

## Current Vision Boundaries

This document supports long-term product definition.

It does not authorise:

* implementation;
* Unity project creation;
* technical architecture;
* asset purchases;
* external integrations;
* public communication;
* final branding;
* commercial commitments.

Those require separate review and approval.
