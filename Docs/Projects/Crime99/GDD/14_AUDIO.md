## Purpose

This document defines audio priorities, impact goals, prototype scope, and long-term direction.

## Audio Principle

Audio variety is a lower prototype priority.

Impact quality is not.

The most important actions must sound:

- heavy;
- bass-driven;
- immediate;
- readable;
- arcade-like.

## Prototype Audio Scope

Required:

- one music track;
- pistol sound;
- shotgun sound;
- rocket launcher sound;
- hit sounds;
- armour-hit sound;
- vehicle impact sounds;
- roadkill impact;
- explosion;
- chain-reaction layering;
- one engine sound per vehicle class;
- burning;
- basic city ambience;
- basic police audio.

## Weapon Audio

## Pistol

Should sound:

- reliable;
- sharp;
- readable;
- lighter than the other weapons.

## Shotgun

Should sound:

- heavy;
- close;
- dangerous;
- bass-rich.

## Rocket Launcher

Should communicate:

- launch;
- travel where useful;
- impact;
- large explosive threat.

## Impact Audio

Vehicle collisions should distinguish:

- light scrape;
- normal collision;
- heavy collision;
- pedestrian impact;
- refuse-truck impact.

Roadkills require strong feedback without realistic gore emphasis.

## Explosion Audio

Explosions should feel powerful.

Audio should support:

- initial blast;
- low-frequency weight;
- debris response;
- secondary chain events;
- distance.

Chain reactions should not become an undifferentiated wall of sound.

## Vehicle Audio

Each prototype class should have a distinct engine identity.

## Compact Car

- light;
- responsive;
- higher-pitched.

## Saloon

- balanced;
- familiar;
- controlled.

## Van

- heavier;
- lower;
- slower response.

## Refuse Truck

- deep;
- industrial;
- mechanical;
- heavy.

Vehicle audio may also include:

- idle;
- acceleration;
- braking;
- handbrake;
- damage;
- engine strain;
- burning.

## City Ambience

Prototype ambience may include:

- distant traffic;
- industrial hum;
- rain or wet-city texture;
- steam;
- distant sirens;
- urban background noise.

Ambience should support atmosphere without masking gameplay.

## Police Audio

Prototype police audio may include:

- siren;
- alert;
- weapon fire;
- pursuit cue.

Police audio should make pressure understandable.

## Music

The prototype requires one track.

The music should support:

- urban tension;
- speed;
- dirt;
- neon;
- criminal energy.

The long-term soundtrack identity may draw from:

- synth-driven night atmosphere;
- industrial texture;
- driving rhythm;
- dark urban mood.

No specific artist imitation should be required.

## Audio Priority Order

1. explosions and roadkills;
2. weapon impact;
3. vehicle class distinction;
4. police readability;
5. city ambience;
6. music variety;
7. secondary environmental sounds.

## Dynamic Audio

Possible later systems:

- music intensity;
- wanted-state layers;
- district ambience;
- speed-responsive mix;
- faction-war escalation.

Not required for the prototype.

## Audio Readability

The player should recognise:

- incoming vehicle;
- nearby explosion;
- burning danger;
- police approach;
- armour break;
- weapon type;
- successful creative kill.

The mix should prioritise immediate danger.

## Audio Success Criteria

Audio succeeds when:

- hits feel physical;
- roadkills have strong impact;
- explosions feel large;
- vehicle classes sound different;
- police pressure is recognisable;
- the mix remains readable during chaos;
- the single music track supports the tone.

## Audio Failure Conditions

Revise if:

- weapons sound weak;
- explosions lack low-frequency weight;
- engine sounds feel interchangeable;
- police audio dominates constantly;
- chain reactions become noise;
- ambience masks gameplay;
- the game only feels powerful when visual effects are shown.

## Deferred Audio Features

- radio stations;
- licensed music;
- dynamic soundtrack;
- dialogue;
- civilian voice sets;
- faction voices;
- detailed vehicle interiors;
- advanced acoustic simulation;
- multilingual voice production.

## Authorisation Boundary

This document defines audio direction.

It does not authorise:

- audio purchases;
- music licensing;
- sound production;
- middleware integration;
- production work.

Those actions require separate approval.
