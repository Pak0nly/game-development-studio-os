# Governance Lifecycle

This document defines how governance ideas move from initial discussion to official adoption within the Studio Operating System.

Its purpose is to prevent premature rules, reduce governance debt, avoid conflicting standards, and ensure that every adopted standard provides long-term value.

## 1. Idea

An idea enters the Governance Lifecycle when it has potential long-term impact on how the Studio Operating System works.

A Governance Idea is not a normal feature request, implementation task, asset request, or project-specific preference. It must affect at least one of the following:

- recurring workflows
- AI behavior
- decision-making
- risk management
- quality standards
- collaboration between human and AI
- cross-project reusability
- security, maintainability, or operational efficiency

Ideas that only affect a single project, feature, asset, scene, or implementation task should remain in the relevant project backlog instead of entering the Governance Lifecycle.

## 2. Discussion

During Discussion, a Governance Idea is clarified before it becomes a backlog item or an adopted rule.

The purpose of this phase is to understand the idea, its intended benefit, its risks, and whether it actually belongs in the Studio Operating System.

Discussion should answer the following questions:

- What problem does this idea solve?
- Is this problem recurring or likely to recur?
- Does this idea affect more than one project?
- Does this idea improve clarity, quality, security, maintainability, collaboration, or operational efficiency?
- Could this idea create unnecessary complexity, governance debt, or operational cost?
- Is there a simpler way to solve the same problem?

A Governance Idea should not move forward if it is unclear, purely project-specific, redundant, or not valuable enough to justify becoming part of the Studio Operating System.

## 3. Backlog

A Governance Idea enters the Governance Backlog when it is considered potentially valuable but not yet ready to become an adopted standard.

The Governance Backlog is a holding area for proposals, not a list of active rules.

A backlog item should be recorded when at least one of the following is true:

- the idea may provide long-term value
- the idea needs further evaluation
- the idea depends on future project experience
- the idea may solve a recurring problem
- the idea is useful but not urgent
- the idea is not yet clear enough for adoption

A backlog item should include enough context to be understood later without relying on memory from the original discussion.

Backlog items must not be treated as official Studio Operating System rules until they pass Evaluation and Adoption.

## 4. Evaluation

During Evaluation, a Governance Backlog item is reviewed to determine whether it should become an adopted Studio Operating System standard.

Evaluation should be evidence-based and should consider long-term value, risk reduction, maintainability, clarity, and operational cost.

A backlog item should be evaluated against the following questions:

* Does this solve a real or likely recurring problem?
* Does it provide long-term value across multiple projects?
* Does it improve decision-making, quality, security, maintainability, collaboration, or efficiency?
* Is the rule or process clear enough to be followed consistently?
* Does it avoid unnecessary complexity?
* Does it create additional operational cost, and is that cost justified?
* Does it conflict with existing principles, definitions, or adopted standards?
* Is there a simpler or more robust alternative?

A backlog item should only move toward Adoption when its expected long-term value is greater than its added complexity and operational cost.

If the value is uncertain, the item should remain in the Governance Backlog until more evidence or project experience is available.


## 5. Adoption

During Adoption, an evaluated Governance Backlog item becomes an official Studio Operating System standard.

Adoption should only happen when the item has been reviewed, provides clear long-term value, and does not create unjustified complexity, conflict, or operational cost.

An adopted standard should be written into the most appropriate official document, such as a principle document, rule document, dictionary, lifecycle document, or project-independent workflow document.

Before adoption, the following must be clear:

* what the standard requires
* why it exists
* where it belongs
* who or what it affects
* when it applies
* whether it replaces, updates, or conflicts with any existing standard

Adopted standards must be clear enough to guide future decisions without relying on memory from the original discussion.

Once adopted, the item is no longer a proposal. It becomes part of the Studio Operating System and should be followed unless it is later changed through the Governance Lifecycle.

## 6. Validation

During Validation, an adopted Studio Operating System standard is checked against real usage.

The purpose of this phase is to confirm that the standard works in practice, not only in theory.

Validation should consider whether the adopted standard:

* is understandable during real work
* is actually followed by humans and AI agents
* improves clarity, quality, security, maintainability, collaboration, or efficiency
* creates unexpected complexity or operational cost
* causes confusion, conflicts, or unnecessary friction
* still supports the original problem it was meant to solve

A standard does not need to be perfect to remain adopted, but it must remain useful, understandable, and justified.

If validation shows that a standard is unclear, ignored, harmful, redundant, or too costly, it should be reviewed again through the Governance Lifecycle.

## 7. Governance Audit

During Governance Audit, the Studio Operating System is reviewed for governance quality, consistency, and long-term health.

The purpose of this phase is to detect problems that may not be visible during day-to-day work.

A Governance Audit may look for:

* governance debt
* governance drift
* conflicting standards
* redundant definitions
* missing rules for recurring problems
* outdated standards
* unclear ownership or decision authority
* security, maintainability, or quality gaps
* rules that create more cost than value

Governance Audit should not automatically create new rules.

Findings from an audit should become Governance Ideas or Governance Backlog items unless they require immediate correction.

## 8. Continuous Improvement

During Continuous Improvement, the Studio Operating System evolves based on validated experience.

The purpose of this phase is to keep the system useful, lightweight, and aligned with real development practice.

Continuous Improvement may lead to:

* refining existing standards
* removing obsolete standards
* resolving conflicts
* simplifying processes
* improving documentation
* adding missing guidance
* updating terminology
* moving unclear ideas back into Discussion or Backlog

The Studio Operating System should improve over time without becoming unnecessarily complex.

Every change should preserve the core goal of governed autonomy: enabling effective AI-assisted solo development through clear, accountable, and maintainable rules.
