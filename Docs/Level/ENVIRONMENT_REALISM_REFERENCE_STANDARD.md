# Environment Realism Reference Standard

## Purpose

This standard establishes how real-world reference supports believable environments without turning reference into unapproved product direction or literal copying. Realism means coherent cause, scale, use, and maintenance appropriate to the project's stylisation.

## Required Reference Record

- place and location type, region/climate where relevant, era, and intended social/economic use;
- source links/citations or locally authorised reference identifiers;
- street profiles, lane/parking/loading patterns, pavement and crossing logic;
- building spacing, height rhythm, entrances, setbacks, fronts, sides, roofs, and rear/service relationships;
- mixed uses, yards, waste, utilities, deliveries, parking, and maintenance states;
- traffic and pedestrian desire lines, conflict points, accessibility, and time-of-day changes;
- landmarks, sightlines, material/weather response, lighting sources, and signage logic;
- environmental-story causes: who uses the place, what changed, what is repaired, neglected, wet, dirty, secured, or abandoned;
- explicit project adaptation: what to keep, simplify, exaggerate, relocate, or avoid.

## Review Rules

1. Use multiple sources where a single image may be exceptional or misleading.
2. Distinguish confirmed observation, inference, and project adaptation.
3. Do not introduce focal period, cultural, legal, or product claims without approved direction.
4. Validate scale and use from reachable gameplay views, not only aerial plans.
5. Ensure service backs, parking/loading, utilities, and boundaries are as intentional as public fronts.
6. Preserve gameplay, camera, navigation, and performance needs while documenting the realism trade-off.

## Modular Environments

Reference must inform edge contracts, building and prop slots, transitions, variants, sightlines, services, and repetition rules. Joined modules must preserve credible continuous streets and uses; seams may not expose a random construction-kit appearance.
